---
title: "Nobody claps for the fire that never started"
date: 2026-07-20
tags: [leadership]

I've worked for leaders who put small problems out while they were still small, and leaders who waited for the inferno so they could be seen fighting it. The second kind often does better. Understanding why is one of the more useful things I've learned in twenty years of infrastructure.

Picture the mechanism, because it's the same every time. A problem starts as a wisp of smoke. At that moment it is trivial to deal with — a conversation, a config change, an afternoon of work, a single decision made early. A leader walks past it. Not because they can't see it. They can see it perfectly well. They walk past because dealing with it now earns nothing. There is no audience for a fire that never happens. Nobody schedules a celebration for the incident that didn't occur.

So the smoke becomes flames, and the flames become an inferno. And now the same leader arrives, in full view, with the hose. Late nights. A war room. Status updates to senior people who are suddenly paying attention. The problem is genuinely hard now, so the effort is genuinely visible, and when it's finally out there is a story to tell — a story with a hero in it. Credit flows. Promotions follow.

The arithmetic is perverse and worth stating plainly. The early fix was cheap and the inferno was ruinous — in cost, in disruption, in risk carried while it burned. But the reward structure is inverted. The person who prevented the fire is invisible, because you cannot point at an absence. The person who "saved" everyone from the fire is decorated, because you can absolutely point at a rescue.

Here is what that inversion does to a technical organisation:

• **It rewards drama over prevention.** The engineer who quietly patches the thing before it breaks has, by definition, no incident to show for it. Their best work looks like a quiet week. The one who lets it break and then heroically restores service has a narrative, a timeline, and a visible win.

• **It turns leadership into a checkbox exercise.** When appearances are what get rewarded, people optimise for appearances. The meeting happens, the box is ticked, the slide is green — and the underlying problem is untouched, because touching it early was never the thing being measured.

• **It quietly punishes your most valuable people.** The person who kept things from ever catching fire is the one you most want to keep, and the one your reward system is least equipped to see. Do this for long enough and they stop bothering, or they leave.

I won't pretend I always fought this cleanly. I learned a tactic in that environment that I'll describe honestly, because it's real and it works. When I could not win an argument on its merits — when the smoke was obvious to me and invisible to the person who needed to act — I stopped pitching harder. Pitching harder made it worse; the more I pushed, the less they bought. So instead I would agree. "That's a good idea, I'll make it happen." Then I'd implement the fix I'd been arguing for all along, and I'd frame it so it felt like their idea, arrived at their way.

Call it what it is: pragmatic stakeholder management. It is not the collaboration I'd want in an ideal team, and I'm not romanticising it. But the goal was never to win the argument. The goal was to get the small fire dealt with while it was still small, and if the price of that was letting someone else feel ownership of the decision, that was a price worth paying every single time. The system rewarded appearances; I used appearances to buy the outcome. The fire went out early. Nobody clapped. That was the point.

None of this is about one workplace or one person. It's a shape you'll find in almost any organisation once appearances become the currency of advancement — and it's set entirely by what leadership chooses to reward. If you celebrate rescues, you'll get rescues. If you want prevention, you have to learn to see, and to reward, the things that never happened.

The best infrastructure work is invisible by design. The best leaders make a point of noticing it anyway.

So here's my question for anyone who runs a team: when was the last time you gave real credit to someone for a problem that never occurred — and if you can't remember, what is your reward system actually training your best people to do?
