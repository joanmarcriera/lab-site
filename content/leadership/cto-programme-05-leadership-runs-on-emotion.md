title: "Leadership runs on emotion, even in a room full of engineers"

I spent most of my career in scientific infrastructure believing that good decisions win arguments. Present the benchmark, show the cost model, the room updates. When that failed — and it failed often — I filed it under "politics" and moved on.

What reframed those failures for me was going back to a result I half-remembered from cognitive science: the [Wason selection task](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wason_selection_task). Give people an abstract logic puzzle about cards and rules and most fail it. Dress the identical logic up as a social situation — someone might be cheating on a rule — and most people solve it without effort. [Leda Cosmides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leda_Cosmides) and John Tooby's interpretation is that human reasoning evolved for social exchange. We are not general-purpose logic machines with feelings bolted on. We are social machines that occasionally do logic.

Antonio Damasio's clinical work, laid out in [Descartes' Error](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descartes%27_Error), completes the picture. Patients whose emotional circuitry was disconnected from their reasoning — the famous [Phineas Gage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phineas_Gage) case and its modern successors — did not become perfectly rational. They became unable to decide anything at all. Every memory we store carries an emotional tag, and those tags do the heavy lifting of judgement long before conscious reasoning shows up to write the press release.

Once you accept that, three practical consequences follow for anyone leading technical teams.

### Your team is running cheat-detection on you

If our brains have specialised machinery for spotting cheaters, then your organisation is a massively parallel cheat-detection cluster pointed at its leadership. This is why announcements are so weak and behaviour so loud. Say "my door is always open" and then check email while someone is talking to you, and the machinery flags it. Say "we want your feedback" after the decision is already made, and it flags that too.

I now treat staff scepticism as data rather than disloyalty. In a long-lived institution, people have watched initiatives come and go; their suspicion of the next glossy programme is well-earned calibration. You do not argue people out of calibrated priors. You feed them new evidence, repeatedly, until the priors move. In infrastructure terms: trust is rebuilt by uptime, not by status pages.

### Reciprocity is capital — and it curdles into cronyism

Robert Trivers described [reciprocal altruism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocal_altruism) in 1971: cooperation between unrelated individuals works when interactions repeat, memory is good, and cheaters can be punished. The emotions he predicted as the enforcement mechanism — gratitude, guilt, liking, moral outrage — are precisely the ones that hold engineering teams together. It is why the colleague who once bailed you out at 3am can ask you for almost anything, and why nothing enrages a team like a freeloader who gets away with it.

Leaders accumulate this capital at scale, and the dark side deserves equal billing. Relationship-based dealing shades into cronyism gradually: first you answer your allies' tickets faster, then you route the interesting projects to people who owe you, and eventually you are a hostage to your own obligation network. History offers spectacular public examples — from politicians jailed for steering contracts to friends, to the well-documented contrast between [Chrysler's cosy, innovation-rich supplier relationships](https://hbr.org/1996/07/how-chrysler-created-an-american-keiretsu) and the ruthless procurement regimes that replaced that model across the industry, which cut costs and then quietly cost manufacturers their suppliers' best ideas. Both failure modes are real: all relationships and you get corruption; no relationships and you get compliance without generosity.

My own audit question: whose requests do I answer first, and would I be comfortable explaining the pattern out loud? At EMBL-EBI, proximity was the silent criterion — the people in our own buildings got the fastest service. Nobody decided that. The reciprocity machinery decided it for us.

### Followers have emotional needs; strategy does not exempt you

Four things I have watched followers need from a leader, and I have been unable to stop seeing them since I first wrote them down: to feel acknowledged as individuals; to be part of something bigger than themselves; to have a genuine relationship with the leader; and to be winning — or at least on a credible path towards it.

The one that stung was acknowledgement. Typing emails while a team member talks to you feels like efficiency. Cumulatively, it is how trust dies — and given the cheat-detection point above, nobody is fooled by performative listening either. The relationship has to be real, because the machinery on the other side is very good at detecting lip service.

The "winning" need explains something every SRE leader has seen: cohesion is mostly the result of success, not its precondition. Teams do not bond their way into shipping; they ship their way into bonding. Your job is to find a winnable early battle — one migration, one dashboard the scientists actually thank you for — and let momentum do the team-building.

### Trainable, like a sport

The most encouraging thing I have learned in this territory: emotional intelligence — knowing your own triggers, managing your reactions, reading others — is learnable in the way a sport is learnable. Not from a book, but through practice and feedback. It took me embarrassingly deep into my career to accept that I am an emotional being at work at all; engineering culture actively rewards pretending otherwise for the first decade.

I have started small: naming my own triggers (mine involve people defending a legacy system with vibes instead of data), pausing before responding to anything that spikes me, and asking in one-to-ones what actually made someone's week better or worse. None of it is sophisticated. All of it works better than another slide of benchmarks.

The strategy deck matters. But it is read by brains that decided how they feel about you long before slide one.
