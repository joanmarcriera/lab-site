No organisation innovates alone any more — [open innovation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_innovation) stopped being a slogan and became a description years ago. Years of vendor deals, collaborations and open-source work have changed how I read a contract, and the conclusion comes first: the financial terms of a partnership are the least interesting part of it. The interesting parts are where the decision rights sit and whether the collaboration architecture lets ideas travel at all.

### Innovation is distributed whether you like it or not

Start with an uncomfortable fact: creativity in organisations is not normally distributed. It is exponential. Most people produce little measurable inventive output; a tiny number produce an enormous amount. You cannot hire your way to the fat end of that curve — but networks shift the whole curve upward. Connected people are more inventive than isolated ones, which means the architecture of collaboration is a leadership deliverable, not an HR nicety.

Four barriers stop ideas travelling, and I recognise every one from years in infrastructure teams: status (we do not respect them, so we do not listen), hoarding (knowledge is power, so it stays put), search (we do not even know what the other team does), and transfer (no shared language or channel even when goodwill exists). I wrote about the silo tax earlier in this series; these four barriers are the itemised bill.

The well-known distributed-innovation stories — Huawei running parallel technology bets across labs on multiple continents, L'Oréal pushing R&D out of Paris into the markets it wanted to win — share one design choice: decision power moved to the edge. The centre funded and connected; it did not adjudicate every bet. EMBL, the intergovernmental research institution where I work, is itself a six-site European laboratory, and I can confirm the corollary from the inside: distributing people without distributing decisions just gives you slower meetings across more time zones.

### The contract menu, and what each item really buys

Walk through the standard contract types and the framing worth keeping is that every contract is a risk-allocation device first and a payment schedule second.

Fixed price puts overrun risk on the vendor — fine for well-understood work, poisonous for anything exploratory, because the vendor's rational move under uncertainty is to cut scope quietly. Cost-plus (time and materials) moves risk to the client, usually capped by a not-to-exceed clause; you gain transparency and lose cost certainty. Incentive or performance-based contracts bolt bonuses and penalties onto either base.

The counter-intuitive finding: high-powered incentives — where the bonus dominates the payment — can degrade performance on creative work. [Ariely, Gneezy, Loewenstein and Mazar showed this experimentally](https://www.bostonfed.org/publications/research-department-working-paper/2005/large-stakes-and-big-mistakes.aspx): past a point, large contingent rewards make performers worse, not better. Under a big contingent payment, the vendor stops exploring and retreats to tried-and-tested approaches. The bonus buys you conservatism. Psychology has said this about individuals for decades; it evidently scales to firms.

For long-horizon, high-failure-risk work, milestone-and-royalty structures — upfront payment, committed funding, payments at defined gates, royalties on success — spread risk across time. Biotech licensing runs on them. IT mostly does not, though I suspect more of our large "transformation" contracts should.

### Control rights: the clause that outlives the invoice

A case I keep coming back to: a small company choosing between handing its product to a large partner (no further costs, smaller share, zero say) or co-developing with a peer (shared costs, bigger upside, joint decisions). You can spreadsheet the expected values — I did — and the numbers are the first step. They must not be the last.

The real question is control rights — roadmap decisions, termination decisions, ownership of derivative technology, tie-breaking votes. Hand your product to a partner with hundreds of parallel alliances and your biggest risk is not failure; it is de-prioritisation for reasons that have nothing to do with you. The research goes a step darker: [Lerner and Merges studied biotechnology alliances](https://www.nber.org/papers/w6014) and found that firms negotiating from weak funding positions concede more control rights — and those deals underperform. Bad timing produces bad governance produces bad outcomes.

I now apply a simple test to any major vendor or platform decision: if this partner loses interest in eighteen months, what can they unilaterally do to us? If the answer is "reshape our roadmap", the price was not the price.

One elegant counter-example I came across: a scale-up that, rather than take money from any single dominant partner, built a membership-based early-access scheme with three large customers — deliberately three, so no single member could capture the roadmap. Control-rights engineering as a funding strategy.

### Ecosystems, and the open-source mirror

Then there are ecosystems: kickstarting one when no rational partner wants to join an empty platform, growing it through standards and shared knowledge, monetising through licences, transaction fees or services while owning some keystone element. Employees, meanwhile, need to learn [coopetition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coopetition) — cooperating and competing with the same partner, often in the same quarter.

Here scientific infrastructure has a head start. Open-source communities are ecosystems in their purest form: no contracts, only control rights, earned through contribution. Anyone who has watched a project's steering committee knows that governance — not code — is where the real battles happen. The ecosystem playbook mapped onto my open-source experience almost one-to-one, which I found either validating or mildly deflating, depending on the day.

### What I took away

• Creativity is exponentially distributed; networks lift the whole curve. Build the connections, not just the team.
• Diagnose collaboration failures against the four barriers before proposing another reorg.
• Read contracts as risk allocation. Fixed price for the known; cost-plus for the uncertain; suspicion for any bonus large enough to change behaviour.
• Negotiate control rights as hard as price. Timing your deal from strength is part of the deal.
• Manage alliances as a portfolio with exits. A partnership no longer serving its purpose is a cost centre with a logo.

The invoice is paid once. The governance clause is paid every quarter, forever.
