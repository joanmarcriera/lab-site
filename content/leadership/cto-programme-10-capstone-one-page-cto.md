Every technology strategy I have watched die, died of length. Forty slides survive the meeting and then dissolve into a folder of PDFs everyone means to reread. So the exercise I now ask of every technology leader I mentor — and repeat myself, on paper, once a year — is deliberately brutal: put your function's next three years on one page.

The format steals shamelessly from the one-page canvas tradition that [Alexander Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_Model_Canvas) made respectable: a fixed set of boxes, filled in about your own organisation, in your own handwriting. Mine has four. This is the last entry in my ten-part series on technology leadership, so it seems right to close by putting the whole series on one page too — because each box quietly audits a theme the earlier parts covered.

### Four blocks, no hiding

**Technology and innovation scope.** Which new technologies must you consider? Does your innovation portfolio need rebalancing? Do your roadmaps need altering — and, pointedly, does your own leadership approach need to change to support any of that? This is the strategy material from earlier in the series (portfolios and measurement, parts 2 and 3) wearing work clothes. It is remarkably easy to hold forth on portfolio theory. It is harder to write down which of your own projects is in the wrong bucket.

**Assets and partnerships.** What capabilities, equipment, skills or capital do you need that you do not have? Which relationships need starting or adapting — and which existing partners no longer serve their intended purpose? That last clause deserves its italics. Everything I wrote in part 8 about alliance portfolios and control rights ends here, in a column where you name names. I find the exit column the slowest to fill of the entire page, every year. Ending a partnership, or retiring a beloved platform, has all the political cost of starting one and none of the ribbon-cutting.

**Processes and governance.** How do the assets and relationships actually deliver the scope? What must change in your processes, your structure — and in the underlying assumptions of your culture? The block deliberately links governance to culture, and the ordering matters: diagnose the culture (parts 4 to 6) before you redesign the machinery (part 7). Process changes that contradict unspoken cultural assumptions are not changes; they are announcements.

**Money and measures.** What is the financial case, and are your current performance measures even fit to track it? After years of arguing about operational metrics, this question lands differently: if the strategy changes and the KPIs do not, the KPIs win. They always win.

### The rule that makes it work

The rule I attach when I hand this exercise to someone: this is a translation exercise — your thought and reflection is what matters, not the final product.

That sentence is the whole point. The canvas is not a deliverable to be polished; it is an instrument for noticing where your thinking is thin. Mine was thin in predictable places. The scope quadrant filled itself; after years of writing strategy plans (the CTO-to-CEO plan in part 7, the IAM product cycle in part 9), I had material queued up. The financial quadrant was serviceable — the finance groundwork I wrote about in another series carried it. The gaps were exits and succession-of-partnerships: the discipline of stopping things. Years of running infrastructure, and the single clearest personal finding this page keeps handing me is that I am better at starting than at stopping. I suspect I am not alone in the profession.

### The South Pole, again

When people push back on the format — surely a real strategy needs more than a page? — I reach for the [race to the South Pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparison_of_the_Amundsen_and_Scott_expeditions): Amundsen's methodical preparation against Scott's improvisation. I have written about it in a standalone post, because it deserved one. Here it plays a specific role: it is a story about execution discipline under uncertainty, and the question it leaves you with is Amundsen's. What do you pack, what do you practise, and what do you leave behind because it is heavy and merely comforting?

A one-page canvas is a packing exercise. That is why it is hard, and that is why it works.

### Closing the series

Ten entries in, a summary of the summary. What a decade and a half of running infrastructure — HPC, scientific computing, consultancy, and finally leading IT services at EMBL-EBI — distils into:

- **A sequence, not a toolbox.** Strategy before culture, culture before execution, execution before partnerships, partnerships before product. Each stage assumes the previous one. Most leadership failure I have witnessed comes from running these out of order — reorganising before diagnosing, partnering before knowing what you need.
- **The habit of writing to my own organisation.** Every framework in this series earned its place the same way: applied to my institute, my services, my stakeholders. The ones I still use are the ones that survived contact with EMBL-EBI's reality.
- **Permission to treat leadership as a discipline.** Engineers respect practice. Stakeholder maps, cascades, personas and canvases are to leadership what runbooks and post-mortems are to operations: unglamorous, repeatable, and the difference between intention and outcome.
- **A healthy scepticism.** Not every celebrated case survives contact with a publicly funded scientific institute, and I have flagged the overreaches throughout this series. A body of ideas that trains you to argue with it has, in my view, worked.

The canvas is on my wall. The exit column is still the shortest. Working on it.
