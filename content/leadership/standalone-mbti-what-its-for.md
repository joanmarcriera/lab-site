Verdict first: the MBTI is scientifically shaky, and mine was still worth the hour it took.

In September 2021 I completed the [Myers-Briggs Type Indicator](https://www.myersbriggs.org/my-mbti-personality-type/myers-briggs-overview/) with the accompanying Communication Style Report, as part of a leadership development push alongside my infrastructure work. I came out as ESTJ — Extraversion, Sensing, Thinking, Judging. If you know the stereotype, that is the organiser: task-focused, structured, direct, fond of a plan and slightly suspicious of meetings without agendas.

If you have worked with me, you are probably nodding.

### The caveat up front

I run infrastructure for a living; I take data quality seriously. So, honestly: the MBTI's [test-retest reliability is contested](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myers%E2%80%93Briggs_Type_Indicator) — a substantial share of people get a different type on retaking it — the binary letters carve continuous traits into artificial halves, and no one should use it for hiring, promotion or team composition. Psychologists tend to prefer other models with stronger evidence behind them.

And yet. The point of a communication-style report is not measurement precision. It is structured self-observation. Its claims are checkable against your own lived experience, and mine checked out often enough to be useful. Treat it as a well-organised interview with yourself, not a lab result.

One detail made it more useful, not less: the preference clarity scores. My Extraversion and Sensing came out as slight preferences — barely over the line — while Thinking was emphatic. The four letters look absolute; the underlying picture is not. Anyone waving their four letters around as an identity has missed the small print on page three of their own report.

### What it got right about me

Three observations from the report earned their keep, because each one changed how I behave.

**I respond fast, talk readily, and can interrupt.** The report politely lists "overlap airspace and interrupt others" among Extraversion tendencies. In incident response this trait is a feature: someone has to make the call and keep the room moving. In design discussions it is a bug. The engineers with the deepest insight are frequently the ones who need four seconds of silence before speaking, and a fast talker removes those four seconds without noticing. The practical fix I adopted was mechanical, not spiritual: in reviews, I ask the quietest person first, and I write my opinion down instead of saying it, so it can wait.

**I want just the facts, applied now.** The Sensing profile describes preferring the practical, the proven, the step-by-step — and being less comfortable with long-term, speculative discussion. That is a fair description of an operations brain. It served me well running production services, where "has this been tried and proven" is the right first question. It served me less well the first time I had to write a multi-year technology strategy, where the right first question is "what could be true in three years". Knowing the bias exists lets me compensate deliberately: I now force myself to write the future-state section of any proposal before the implementation section, because the reverse order plays to my comfort and shortchanges the thinking.

**I default to statements; questions work better.** The Thinking and Judging combination produces decisiveness, frank feedback and closure — and, unmanaged, a person who ends discussions rather than opens them. The single best line in the report was the suggestion to accompany opinions with questions. I tried it, sceptically, in one-to-ones. The difference was immediate. "I think we should migrate this service" gets compliance or silent disagreement. "What would break if we migrated this?" gets information. Same opinion, better outcome.

### What these reports are actually for

Here is the thing the MBTI industry undersells and the MBTI critics overlook: for a working manager, the value of the report is not the type. It is that it puts precise, neutral vocabulary on behaviour your colleagues have already noticed and will rarely tell you about.

Nobody on my teams was going to say "you overlap airspace". The PDF said it. Once I had the words, I could watch for the behaviour, and once I could watch for it, I could change it. That loop — external description, self-observation, small mechanical adjustment — is the entire practical content of most leadership development, dressed in various frameworks. This particular framework got me there for the price of a questionnaire.

Three rules I would offer anyone handed one of these reports:

• Read it looking for the two or three claims that sting slightly. Those are the useful ones. The flattering pages are decoration.
• Convert each sting into a mechanical habit, not an aspiration. "Listen more" fails; "ask the quietest person first" works.
• Never weaponise it. The moment types are used to explain other people ("she's such a P, of course the plan slipped") the tool has become an instrument for laziness.

The sixteen boxes are a fiction. The mirror is real. Use the mirror, ignore the boxes.
