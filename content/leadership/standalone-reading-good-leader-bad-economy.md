title: "The recession playbook that makes the recession worse"
date: 2026-07-19T17:20:00+01:00

---

There is a standard playbook for leading through a downturn, and most of us reach for it on instinct. Get closer to the team. Set a faster pace. Ask people to carry more. Every one of those moves is defensible. Merete Wedell-Wedellsborg's argument in ["How to Be a Good Leader in a Bad Economy"](https://hbr.org/2022/11/how-to-be-a-good-leader-in-a-bad-economy) is that the playbook is right about direction and dangerously wrong about dose — and that a leader running it at full throttle can turn a difficult quarter into a destructive spiral.

I found this piece more honest than most crisis-leadership writing, because it starts from a specific observation: people who have already been through compounding stress are not more resilient, they are more brittle. The comforting story is that each hardship toughens a team. The reality she describes, and the one I recognise, is the opposite. By the time the budget conversation lands, a lot of people are running closer to empty than their managers assume.

Her structure is three balances. I will take them through the lens of running production infrastructure — services people depended on around the clock, and the ordinary reality of doing that with less money than the roadmap wanted.

### Move closer without suffocating

When money tightens, the first leadership reflex is to move closer. More meetings, more reporting, more detail in every conversation. Psychologically, she notes, this is usually about the leader's need to feel in control rather than the team's need for help.

I have watched this happen and I have done it myself. The trouble is that a team which has learned to operate independently reads a sudden increase in oversight as distrust. Attention shifts from doing the work to managing upward. And the worst version — the leader who takes over a subordinate's task because they are convinced they can do it better — clogs the leader's own bandwidth precisely when clarity is scarce. Her test is a good one: after you have moved closer, does the team still own the list of things to fix, or did you walk out carrying it? The point of getting closer is to motivate and support, not to seize the wheel. Move in, then have an exit.

### Move faster without turning frantic

A bias for action is correct. You cannot sit still in a downturn. But she draws a sharp line between urgent and frantic, and it is a line I have crossed.

The specific trap is the imaginary deadline. "We need this by the end of the month" manufactures urgency, and sometimes urgency is exactly what a value-destroying artificial deadline delivers — a worse solution, shipped on time, when a better one was a few months out. In infrastructure the temptation is acute, because you can always cut a corner to hit a date: skip the migration rehearsal, defer the capacity headroom, postpone the hard conversation about technical debt. It feels like speed. It is borrowing, at a bad interest rate.

Her antidote is impulse control by design: build a deliberate, fast, informal delay into your own decisions. Not bureaucracy — a phone call to someone you trust who will tell you if the plan is mad. Under pressure, leaders tolerate dissent less, read objection as obstruction, and drift toward a false consensus that feels efficient and quietly starves the team of better ideas. A trusted vetting habit is cheap insurance against your own narrowed judgement.

### Add workload without severing relationships

The third reflex is to become more task-focused and less mindful of relationships. The to-do list grows because "more" feels like responsible leadership. Off-sites get cancelled, development gets paused, and empathy is filed under things we cannot afford right now.

This is the one I will underline hardest, because her framing corrected something in my own thinking. Relationship work is not coddling. It is hard-core performance management. People rarely quit or quiet-quit because the job got harder or the times got tougher. They leave because they lose faith — in their leaders, their colleagues, or the direction. When you strip out the connective tissue to save money, you are not trimming luxury, you are removing the main reason good people stay when the work is unpleasant.

You can economise on the format. Fewer five-star settings, sure. But keep the five-star content — the honest conversation about what the team owes each other during a hard stretch, and what compromises you will not make even for a short-term win. If the downturn drags, her advice is to stop and redefine what success looks like with the team, rather than silently raising the bar and wondering why morale collapsed.

### The honest caveat

The article is light on one thing, so I will add it. These balances demand a lot of a leader's own regulation, and that is a finite resource. You cannot run three continuous balancing acts indefinitely while also being personally frightened about the budget. The realistic move is to be explicit with yourself about which of the three you overshoot under stress — for me it is "move closer" — and put a specific check on that one, rather than pretending you will hold all three in perfect tension by willpower.

The deeper lesson is that the standard crisis responses are not wrong. They are natural and legitimate. Left ungoverned, they amplify the very problem they were meant to solve. Leading well in a bad economy is mostly the discipline of doing the obvious things at the right dose — and noticing when your need to feel in control has quietly taken the wheel.

When the money gets tight, which instinct do you have to hold back — closer, faster, or more?
