Whenever an innovation programme stalls, the post-mortem reaches for the comfortable explanations. Not enough budget. The technology was not ready. Leadership did not back it. These are comfortable because they are external — someone else's fault, or nobody's. Scott Kirsner's ["The Biggest Obstacles to Innovation in Large Companies"](https://hbr.org/2018/07/the-biggest-obstacles-to-innovation-in-large-companies) surveyed 270 leaders in strategy, innovation and R&D roles and produced a ranking that quietly demolishes the comfortable stories.

The top obstacle, cited by 55%, was politics, turf wars and a lack of alignment. Second, at 45%, was cultural issues. Third, at 42%, the inability to act on signals crucial to the future of the business. Budget came fourth. Strategy fifth. And at the very bottom, cited by just 10%, was lack of CEO support.

I keep this ranking because it corrects the instinct of most technical leaders, including me. We frame innovation as a resourcing problem — give me headcount and a budget line and I will deliver. The data says the real obstacles are social and political, and they sit beside you, not above you.

### The CEO is not your blocker

Start at the bottom of the list, because it is the most liberating finding. Only one in ten respondents said the CEO was constraining innovation. Kirsner's line is exactly right: the CEO does not wield a sledgehammer that can demolish any obstacle blocking a team with a good idea. If you have been waiting for executive air cover to unstick your initiative, you may be waiting on the wrong person. The friction is lateral.

I have lived this. The times my technical initiatives stalled, it was almost never because someone senior said no. It was because a peer function felt encroached upon, or two teams were quietly competing for the same territory, and the whole thing died of a thousand small non-decisions. Which brings us back to the top of the list.

### Turf is the real enemy

Anything genuinely innovative cuts across boundaries — that is what makes it innovative and what makes it threatening. Some business unit already believes it is doing innovation on its own, and your new initiative reads as edging into its terrain and competing for its resources. Sometimes people simply hope the CEO's new favourite — the shiny Chief Innovation or Chief Digital hire — will go away if ignored long enough.

For a technologist this is a hard reframe, because it is not a problem you can solve with a better architecture. Kirsner's constructive move is that senior leaders may not be able to squash every squabble, but they can be explicit about what the new group is meant to do and how everyone else is expected to support it. The unglamorous work is boundary-drawing and expectation-setting, done out loud, before the turf war starts rather than after.

### Seeing the signal is easy; acting is hard

The finding that most matches my infrastructure experience is the third. The survey split "picking up" signals of change from "acting on" them. Only 18% struggled to notice change — most companies can see the disruptive startup, the shifting customer behaviour, the new technology. The failure, at 42%, is acting on what they already see.

This is the whole game. Awareness is cheap and common. What is missing is the mechanism: when a forward scout spots something important, is there a fast path to run a small pilot with a business unit, or does everything queue for the annual strategy off-site? In technical terms, the organisation's observability is fine and its incident response is broken. I have seen teams write prescient internal memos about a coming shift and then do nothing, not from blindness but from having no cheap route from insight to experiment.

### Budget matters less than you think, and differently

Budget lands fourth, and the nuance is worth it. In industries with mature R&D functions, money is not the constraint. But nearly 40% of respondents ran innovation on under $5m a year, and 23% under $1m. At that level, as one former Nike executive put it, the job stops being innovation and becomes building the case for investing in innovation — trend-scouting and a few demos to unlock more resources later. That is a real and often unspoken trap: a budget too small to do the work, only large enough to justify asking for more.

### What actually helps

The enablers are the encouraging part. The single most-cited one, by more than half of respondents, was the ability to test, learn and iterate — running quick-and-dirty experiments, gathering results, trying again. This is home turf for anyone from an engineering culture. Cheap, fast, reversible experiments are the thing we know how to build. The organisational version is the same discipline pointed at strategy instead of code.

The other enabler is unfashionable: long-term commitment. Cultures reject initiatives they suspect are flavour-of-the-month, and one write-in named the disease perfectly — "Leadership ADHD". Innovation has to be framed as a daily regimen, part of how things are done here from now on, not a magical incantation that delivers results the moment a CEO says the word on an earnings call.

### The takeaway I will actually use

If I strip this to one operating principle: stop framing innovation as a budget request and start framing it as a political and cultural project. Map the turf before you launch. Name the functions whose backyard you are entering and negotiate the boundary in the open. Build the cheap-experiment mechanism, because seeing the future is not the hard part — acting on it is. And do not wait for a sledgehammer from above that, the data says, does not exist.

If the CEO is not the one blocking your best idea, who is — and have you had the honest conversation with them yet?
