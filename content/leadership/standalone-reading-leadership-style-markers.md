Early in my career I turned down leadership roles more than once. Part of it was genuine preference for the technical work. Part of it, if I am honest, was a vague sense that I lacked whatever it was that senior people seemed to have and I did not. Nobody could tell me what it was. The feedback was always the fog: "presence", "gravitas", "intangibles". You cannot act on fog.

["How to Develop Your Leadership Style"](https://hbr.org/2020/11/how-to-develop-your-leadership-style) by Suzanne Peterson, Robin Abramson and R.K. Stutman is the piece I wish I had read then, because it does the one thing all that feedback refused to do: it makes the fog concrete. Their central claim is that leadership style is distinct from personality. Personality is who you are on the inside and it does not really move. Style is what you do — how often, and when. And style is built from observable behaviours they call status markers.

### The two families of markers

Markers fall into two groups. Power markers signal confidence, competence and authority — but overdone, they read as arrogant and abrasive. Interrupting. Grabbing the pen. Longer, faster, louder speech. Abrupt topic shifts. Little note-taking. Attractiveness markers signal warmth, approachability and likability — but overdone, they read as diffident and submissive. Questions instead of statements. Hedging. Deferring. Extensive note-taking. Nodding.

Neither family is good or bad. The insight is that most of us default to one mix in neutral situations — our natural style — and the more consistently we use the same markers, the more distinctive and fixed our reputation becomes. The colleague everyone finds arrogant is usually just running a small set of power markers on repeat.

### Decoding the feedback you actually get

Here is where it becomes a tool. The authors give a translation table, and it is the most useful thing in the article. "You're not senior enough" or "you need more gravitas" almost always means too many attractiveness markers — so use declarative statements, dress for the context, hedge less. "You're intimidating" or "your team is afraid of you" means too many power markers — so speak less, ask more questions, shift topics gradually.

Suddenly the fog resolves. Gravitas is not a substance you either possess or lack. It is a diagnosis: you are leaning attractive in rooms that call for power. That is a behaviour, and behaviours can be rehearsed.

### The mistake almost everyone makes

The counterintuitive bit: people instinctively use power markers with juniors and attractiveness markers with seniors. Ignoring a junior, talking over them, changing the subject — while being deferential and hedging with the boss. The authors argue the opposite is usually more effective. Lean attractive with junior people, because respect and attention are what motivate them. Lean powerful with senior people, because deference read as weakness makes them stop treating you as a peer. Overdoing attractiveness upward — phrasing everything as a question, trailing off with "um" — is especially likely to backfire.

I have watched capable engineers cap their own careers exactly here. They are generous and collaborative with peers and juniors, which is lovely, and then they walk into the room with the directors and shrink. The technical content is flawless. The markers say "not one of us."

### The uncomfortable part they do not dodge

The article is honest about something a lot of leadership writing skips: markers are not read neutrally. The same behaviour is judged differently depending on who does it. A woman who disagrees is "abrasive"; a man doing the identical thing is "candid". Minority and LGBTQ leaders who do not match the dominant culture get quietly penalised as "not like us". The authors do not pretend a clever set of markers dissolves bias. They treat the blended style as a way to navigate a field that is not level — walking a narrower tightrope, with less margin for error. Their example is Ruth Bader Ginsburg, whose "disagree agreeably" style built alliances across deep disagreement. I would rather the tightrope did not exist. It does, and naming it honestly is more useful than pretending otherwise.

### Why this is not about being fake

The objection I had on first read was: is this just teaching people to perform? The authors answer it directly, and I have come round. Emulating a style, or flexing your own into a wider range, does not make you inauthentic — it means you are growing. You can be genuinely nervous before a big presentation and still choose power markers so the room hears confidence. What you feel inside and what you display are allowed to differ. That is not deception; it is craft. Every good engineer already knows this in another form: you write code for the reader, not to express your inner state.

The practical path they suggest is unglamorous and correct. Diagnose where you sit on the spectrum — ask people, or record yourself in a meeting and count your own markers. Pick one verbal and one nonverbal marker to experiment with. Rehearse. Read the room and match what you receive. Expand slowly.

For anyone who has ever been told they need more of an undefinable something, this is the antidote. The something has a name, it is made of specific behaviours, and unlike your personality, you get to change it.

What is one piece of "you need more X" feedback you have carried around — and which marker does it actually point to?
