There is a moment, some time after you take a leadership role, when you realise the information reaching you has changed shape. Meetings agree with you a little too readily. Bad news arrives late and pre-softened. Your throwaway suggestion in a review comes back three weeks later as a fully built feature nobody actually wanted. It took me longer than I would like to admit to understand what was happening: I had not gotten smarter. I had gotten authority, and authority distorts the room.

That is the premise of ["5 Traps to Avoid as You Gain Power as a Leader"](https://hbr.org/2024/06/5-traps-to-avoid-as-you-gain-power-as-a-leader) by Julie Diamond, Lisa Zigarmi and Lesli Mones, and it is the sharpest thing I have read on the subject. Their core point is not the tired "power corrupts". It is subtler and more useful: power changes the people around you, and their changed behaviour then reshapes you, usually without your consent or awareness. Once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

They name five traps. I will take them through the lens of running technical teams and infrastructure, because a couple of them are practically occupational hazards for people who came up as engineers.

### The Savior trap

Being the identified authority pulls you toward giving advice, having all the answers, and being endlessly helpful. It curdles into being the person who solves everyone's problem, micromanages, and weighs in on things outside their competence.

This one is mine. It is the specific disease of people who got promoted because they were good at fixing things. At EMBL-EBI I ran services other people depended on, and the reflex that made me useful as an engineer — see problem, solve problem — made me a bottleneck as a leader. When you are busy saving everyone, you are a single point of failure, you starve the team of the chance to develop, and you quietly train people to be less accountable because you will always catch it. Their fix is almost embarrassingly simple and it works: ask one question before you give an answer. "What have you tried?" "What do you think the problem is?" It breaks the reflex and hands the problem back to its owner.

### The Complacency trap

The mirror image. Because you are the expert, you stop being curious. You assume you understand the problem, you ask fewer questions, and you read silence as agreement. But silence is often just compliance, and compliance is not consensus.

This is dangerous in infrastructure specifically because so many outages live in the gap between what the senior person assumed and what the system actually does. The authors' remedy is a deliberate practice of inquiry — "what assumptions are we making?", "what haven't we asked?", the old five-whys — and, bluntly, being present. Get off the phone in the one-to-one. Curiosity is the thing you lose first and notice last.

### The Avoidance trap

Power lets you take shortcuts, and the most tempting one is dodging the unpleasant part of the job. The hard conversation. The festering team conflict. The tough feedback you keep meaning to give. Authority makes it easy to outsource or simply skip these.

The cost is not immediate, which is exactly why it is a trap. Avoiding hard things atrophies your ability to do them, and it signals to the team that being accountable to the role is optional. A leader who picks and chooses their duties is read as unreliable, and unreliability is corrosive to trust. Their question — "is it my obligation to do this, and what does my inaction cost?" — is a good one to keep on a sticky note.

### The Friend trap

The opposite failure: leaders uncomfortable with power over-rely on being liked. They act like a peer when they are not, struggle to hold people accountable, sometimes share things they should not or play favourites. This shows up hard when someone is promoted to manage their former teammates.

The consequence is counterintuitive. Not stepping into your authority does not create a warm, flat, happy team. It leaves a vacuum, and the vacuum gets filled by the most dominant person present — not necessarily the most competent. Refusing to lead is itself a decision, and usually a bad one.

### The Stress trap

Leading is stressful, and unmanaged stress does not stay with you. Through the amplifier of your position, it becomes secondhand stress for everyone downstream. The line that stuck with me: everyone is allowed a bad day, but when you are the boss, you think you are being mildly grumpy while the other person experiences you TEXTING IN ALL CAPS. The self-protective result is that people stop bringing you bad news early, which is the last thing you want.

### The honest part

What I appreciate is that the authors do not pretend these are fully avoidable. The role genuinely requires you to have answers, to delegate the grunt work, to be collegial. Every trap is the overshoot of something the job legitimately asks for. There is no clean state of power without them. The discipline is vigilance — noticing when you are leaning on a comfort the position hands you, and reaching for the specific counter-move.

The uncomfortable truth underneath all five is the one I opened with. The feedback loop that would normally correct you — people telling you the truth — is precisely the thing your power degrades. So you have to build the correction deliberately: ask before answering, invite dissent, verify that the quiet in the room is agreement and not surrender.

Which of the five is nearest to your desk this month — and what would asking one more question cost you?
