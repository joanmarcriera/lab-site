When offices emptied, a particular anxiety spread through management: we are going to lose our culture. It was usually expressed through the missing furniture — the watercooler conversations, the corridor run-ins, the perks. Jennifer Howard-Grenville's ["How to Sustain Your Organization's Culture When Everyone Is Remote"](https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/how-to-sustain-your-organizations-culture-when-everyone-is-remote/) was written in the thick of that moment, and it argues, convincingly, that the anxiety mistook the props for the play. Read now, with distributed work a permanent fixture rather than an emergency, it holds up better than most of what was written then.

Her first move is to borrow a distinction from Edgar Schein. The most visible signifiers of culture — the foosball tables, the in-house chefs — are, in [Schein's terms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edgar_Schein), artifacts. They are at best vehicles for the interactions that fuel a real culture of, say, playful creativity. They are not the culture itself. So when the artifacts disappear, as they did overnight, the deeper beliefs and habitual practices need not disappear with them. That is genuinely reassuring, and it reframes the problem. You did not lose your culture when you lost the office. You lost some of its scaffolding, and now you have to hold the structure up a different way.

### Culture as a tool kit

The idea I took most from the piece is her use of the sociologist Ann Swidler, whose ["Culture in Action"](https://www.asanet.org/wp-content/uploads/savvy/introtosociology/Documents/ASRSwidler1986.pdf) describes habitual practices as the core carriers of culture. People draw from a "tool kit" of cultural habits and practices, and knowing how to use those tools — when and how they apply — is the real mark of belonging.

This lands hard for anyone who has led engineers. A team's real culture was never the values poster in the lobby. It is the practices: how code review actually happens, what "done" means, how an incident call is run, whether a post-mortem is honest or theatre. Those are the tools. A new hire becomes one of us not when they memorise the values but when they know which tool to reach for in which situation. And crucially, those practices survive the office. They live in the pull request and the incident channel, not the postcode.

### Three things a remote leader can actually do

Howard-Grenville is refreshingly practical, which is rare in culture writing. She gives three moves.

**Make culture visible by calling it out.** In an office, the practices that carry culture are semi-visible — you catch colleagues interacting, making decisions, reacting. Remote, that is gone, so a manager has to name it. When a team lands on a good approach, say why in cultural terms: we got here because we are so good at drawing on multiple perspectives. Naming it does two jobs — it reminds people the practice exists, and it signals that it is valued. She adds a sharp counterpart: call out affronts too. If practices that violate the desired culture go un-censured, the boundaries of the culture blur. Culture that is only espoused by leaders and never enforced is not real.

**Welcome modifications to the tool kit.** One benefit of thinking in tools is realising you hold more than you routinely use, and that is exactly what lets you use culture flexibly. Sometimes you need to be entrepreneurial; sometimes, in the same organisation, risk-averse. And tool kits change, because people bring in habits from the rest of their lives — the way a Zoom yoga session runs, the way a teacher coaxes input from a reticent teenager. Remote work scattered everyone into those other contexts, so it is a moment to notice good practices arriving from outside and deliberately fold them in. The counterpart matters here too: modifications that undermine core beliefs should be countered quickly, ideally by offering a more culturally-aligned alternative rather than just a no.

**Use disruption to bolster the core.** Not every item in the kit is equally worth guarding. Some are like cheap picture hangers — useful, substitutable. Others are the grandfather's worn measuring tape, valuable because they carry history and identity. Disruption is a chance to retell the founding stories and remind people who the organisation is — and, just as usefully, to quietly retire rituals that were only ceremonial and were holding back changes people actually wanted. Her example is that some long-desired shifts — decentralising decisions, cutting bureaucracy — happened in weeks under pressure that had resisted years of trying. But those changes only stick with ongoing work to connect them to the existing culture.

### The caution, and the honest bit

She adds a warning I would underline: changing aspects of culture that are tied strongly to identity is especially disruptive and generates strong emotions. Not everything that suddenly became possible should be kept, and not everything old should be swept away because it is old. Judgement, not enthusiasm.

And here is the part I would add from running distributed infrastructure teams across time zones for years. The tool-kit view is liberating but it is not free. Practices that carry culture need reinforcement, and remote strips out the ambient, accidental reinforcement the office used to provide. So you have to be deliberate in a way office-based teams never had to be — over-communicating the why, narrating decisions, making the invisible visible on purpose. That is more work, not less. The reward is that culture built this way is more robust, because it lives in explicit practice rather than in proximity.

Her closing line is the one worth keeping: it was never about the watercooler anyway. Culture is the actions we take and make visible to others, and the meaning we invest in them. Harder to sustain from a kitchen table, yes. Not impossible — and, done right, clearer than it ever was in the corridor.

Which of your team's habits deserves promotion from "something nice we do" to "this is how we work here" — and have you said so out loud?
