"Agile" has been worn smooth by overuse. For most organisations it now means a set of ceremonies bolted onto software delivery — standups, sprints, a board of sticky notes — and a quiet suspicion that none of it made the company any more able to change direction when the market moved. So I went into ["Cultivating Strategic Agility"](https://doi.org/10.1002/joe.22241) by Kyriakos Christofi, Pieris Chourides and George Papageorgiou expecting another framework, and came out with one idea I have not been able to put down. Usefully, it is an open-access paper, so anyone can read the whole thing rather than take my summary on trust.

The study is empirical. The authors examined four multinationals across IT, semiconductors and retail, over the decade 2010 to 2020, during which their market capitalisations grew by between 20% and a startling 747%. They interviewed senior strategists and triangulated against a decade of annual reports, press releases and public presentations, looking for the practices that actually distinguish a strategically agile organisation. Strategic agility, in their definition, is the capacity to keep creating value by adjusting strategic direction quickly, without sacrificing efficiency — the ability to turn without falling over.

### The five pillars

They land on five pillars, and taken as a list they will not shock anyone: knowledge management, dynamic talent management, open innovation, digitalization, and sustainability. What makes the paper worth reading is the texture inside each, and one idea in particular that reframes the whole thing.

### Knowledge management, and the part everyone skips

The first pillar is knowledge management, and this is where the paper earned its place in my notes. The obvious half is what you expect: agile organisations are knowledge-centric, good at generating, sharing and applying knowledge fast, through cross-functional teams and continuous education. Fine.

The half that stopped me is unlearning. Drawing on the knowledge-management literature, the authors stress that continuous renewal is not only about generating new knowledge — it entails the intentional discarding of outdated and ineffectual practices. Unlearning, deliberately letting go of obsolete knowledge, is described as pivotal to the journey toward agility.

I have never seen this stated so plainly, and it names something every long-serving infrastructure person knows in their bones. The thing that makes a large technical organisation rigid is rarely a lack of new ideas. It is the accumulated weight of old ones that nobody will retire. The migration everyone dreads because a decade of "the way we do it here" has calcified around the system being replaced. The runbook nobody deletes although the service it documents was decommissioned two years ago. The deployment ritual that made sense on the old platform and is now pure superstition. Adding new practices is easy and feels like progress. Subtracting old ones is politically harder and matters more. If agility has a single unglamorous engine, this is it: an organisation that is as disciplined about what it stops doing as about what it starts.

### Talent as a fluid, not a fixture

The second pillar, dynamic talent management, is about treating people as a fluid resource rather than fixed org-chart furniture. Temporary cross-functional teams. Employees allowed to shape their own career paths. And a deliberate focus on T-shaped expertise — deep in one domain, broad enough to contribute across several. That last one is the right unit for an agile technical team: the engineer who is genuinely expert in storage but can hold a sensible conversation about networking and pick up a service in an unfamiliar area is worth more, in a turning organisation, than a narrow specialist who cannot move.

The authors lean on a plain performance-versus-potential matrix to sort talent — high/high are the succession pipeline, high-potential/low-performance need development, and so on. It is not a novel tool, and I would not oversell it; matrices like this can flatten people into quadrants. But as a prompt for deliberately building mobility and cross-functional skill, rather than letting teams ossify around whoever happened to be hired for what, it does useful work.

### Digitalization, stated bluntly

The digitalization pillar is refreshingly free of mysticism. Cloud computing, automation of high-volume processes, platforms that connect internal functions to external partners, and data used for real-time decisions. One interviewee's line is the one I would quote: they now make strategic decisions from a flood of good data, "instead of a bunch of old men sitting around the table" guessing. For an infrastructure leader there is nothing exotic here — it is the case for the platform work we already believe in, phrased as strategy rather than plumbing. Which is exactly how it should be phrased when you are arguing for the budget.

Open innovation (collaborate outside your walls, with universities and partners, rather than hoarding R&D) and sustainability (ESG as a genuine strategic input, not a compliance afterthought) round out the five. Both are sensible; neither surprised me as much as the unlearning point.

### What I would push back on

Two honest caveats. First, the sample is four large, publicly listed multinationals, and the authors say so — they flag that the findings may not transfer cleanly to SMEs or to private organisations without public ESG data. Much of my own career has been in scientific and infrastructure settings that look nothing like a Fortune 500, and some of these pillars are harder to reach there. Second, there is a survivorship shadow over any study of companies chosen partly because they grew market cap 20–747%. We are looking at winners and inferring what made them win; the same practices in a company that stalled would not headline a paper. Read it as a well-argued hypothesis, not a proof.

But the central reframing survives both caveats, and it is the thing I have taken into how I think about my own teams. Strategic agility is not a process you install and it is not a ceremony you perform on Tuesdays. It is a whole-system property, and its quiet foundation is the willingness to unlearn — to keep deciding, honestly and continually, which of the things you are good at have stopped being worth doing.

What is the one practice your team keeps purely because you always have — and what would it take to retire it?
