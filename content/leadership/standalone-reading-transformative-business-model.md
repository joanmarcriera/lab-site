Spend any time near infrastructure and you develop an allergy to the phrase "this changes everything". A new model, a faster interconnect, a clever protocol — each arrives wrapped in the claim that the technology itself is the revolution. It almost never is. ["The Transformative Business Model"](https://store.hbr.org/product/the-transformative-business-model/R1610H) by Stelios Kavadias, Kostas Ladas and Christoph Loch is the clearest statement I have found of why, and it gave me a checklist I now use on every hype cycle, including this one.

Their opening example is perfect because it is so counterintuitive in hindsight. MP3 technology was a genuine order-of-magnitude leap — thousands of songs on a device that fit in a pocket, versus a CD that held one album. And for years it changed almost nothing about the music industry. Early MP3 players came and went. What actually moved music from the physical to the virtual world was not the codec. It was Apple pairing the iPod with iTunes: a business model that linked the technology to an emerging market need. The authors put it flatly — new technologies have often been major factors, but they have never transformed an industry on their own. What does the transforming is a business model that connects what the technology enables to what the market wants.

### The six features

To find out what makes a business model transformative rather than merely novel, they analysed 40 companies that had launched new models across many industries. Some upended their sectors; some looked promising and fizzled. Six features recurred:

1. **A more personalised product or service** — better tailored to individual, immediate needs, usually at a competitive price.
2. **A closed-loop process** — replacing linear make-use-dispose with recycling, cutting resource cost.
3. **Asset sharing** — unlocking costly assets through two-sided marketplaces. Airbnb shares rooms; Uber shares cars. It also lowers entry barriers, because the entrant need not own the assets.
4. **Usage-based pricing** — charging when the customer uses the thing and gets value, rather than demanding an upfront purchase.
5. **A more collaborative ecosystem** — technology that improves collaboration with partners and allocates risk more sensibly.
6. **An agile, adaptive organisation** — moving away from hierarchical decision-making toward real-time adaptation to market needs.

Each feature is a bridge between a technology trend and a market need. Personalisation answers the fragmentation of consumer preferences, made possible by cheap sensors and data. Asset sharing answers rising input costs. And so on. The features are not a grab bag; they are specific couplings of capability to demand.

### The number that matters: three

The finding I keep is quantitative and blunt. The authors scored each of the 40 models by how many features it beat the incumbent on, then compared that to how much the model actually transformed its industry — measured by market share taken and by how many competitors copied it. Models with transformative potential tended to have three or more of the six features. Below three, the odds of transformation were low. Uber ticks five. Airbnb, Amazon, Google AdWords, IKEA, Zipcar — all four. It is not a guarantee; you cannot guarantee an innovation's success unless you pick a market so small it does not matter. But it loads the dice, and it gives you a way to rate your own idea before you bet on it.

### Why this is the right lens for the AI moment

I find this framework more useful now than when it was written, precisely because we are mid-way through a technology wave where the raw capability is genuinely extraordinary and the temptation to declare victory on the capability alone is overwhelming. A powerful model is the MP3, not the iPod-plus-iTunes. The question the framework forces is the productive one: which of the six features does this capability actually plug into, and through what business model?

Take usage-based pricing, which the authors describe from the customer's side — you incur cost only as the offering generates value. That is exactly the shape of well-designed API and inference pricing, and it is a large part of why cloud AI spread the way it did. Or agility: technology that lets decisions be made in real time, decentralised, instead of climbing a hierarchy. Their own case study, a rare-disease venture called Healx, pushed personalisation to its maximum and added agility by putting a data-and-algorithm decision directly in the clinician's hands rather than relying on fixed rules of thumb. The pattern generalises: the transformation happens when the capability is wired into a model that changes who decides, who pays, and when.

### The honest limits

Two caveats I would add. First, this is a diagnostic, not a recipe. Scoring three-plus tells you the model has transformative potential; it does not tell you the market timing is right, the execution will hold, or a better-funded competitor will not copy you. The authors are careful about this and I will be too. Second, the six features are not equally available in every industry. In heavily regulated, capital-intensive, or trust-dependent domains — the kind of scientific and infrastructure work I have spent my career in — asset sharing and agility can be structurally harder to reach, and personalisation may run straight into privacy and compliance limits. The framework still helps; it just also tells you why some industries are stubborn.

The single habit I have taken from this piece is small and useful. When someone tells me a new technology will change everything, I do not argue about the technology. I ask which of the six features it enables, against which incumbent, and through what model that captures value. If the answer is "well, it is just really good," that is an MP3 player looking for its iTunes. Sometimes it finds one. Usually the person who builds the connecting model wins, not the person who built the capability.

Which of the six does the technology you are most excited about actually connect to — and who is building the model around it?
