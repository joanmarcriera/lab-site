title: "Put your aces on page one: what reviewing a fellow executive's CV taught me about mine"
date: 2026-07-19T17:55:00+01:00
tags: [leadership]
---

One of the quiet benefits of a strong professional network has nothing to do with networking events: you end up swapping CVs with accomplished peers and marking each other's homework. A fellow technology executive asked me to review their CV. I wrote them several paragraphs of feedback. Then I reread my own CV and discovered I had violated most of my own advice.

Here is the advice, generalised, because it applies to nearly every senior technical CV I have seen since — including, at various points, mine.

### Ten seconds. That is the budget.

A screener — human or increasingly otherwise — gives a CV about ten seconds before deciding whether it earns a full read. Ten seconds is the top third of page one. Not the top third of each page: the top third of the first one.

So the first rule I gave my colleague: put your aces there. Whatever the two or three most impressive, most quantified, most differentiating facts of your career are, they go in that window. Everything else on the document exists only to support a decision the reader has mostly already made.

Engineers resist this because it feels like boasting and because chronology feels honest. But a CV is not an audit log; it is an executive summary of a career. The [pyramid principle](https://www.barbaraminto.com/concept.html) — [Barbara Minto's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbara_Minto) rule of conclusion first, supporting detail after — applies to CVs exactly as it applies to board papers. If your strongest evidence appears in the 2016 entry halfway down page two, you have filed your conclusion in an appendix.

### One sticky message

The second flaw I flagged: the CV tried to be everything. Strategy, architecture, delivery, people leadership, security, data, transformation — all present, all equally weighted, no centre of gravity. The reader finishes unsure what they just read.

A strong CV answers "what is this person?" in one sentence, fast. For me, something like: infrastructure leader who scales scientific computing — petabytes, thousands of users, small budgets. Everything on the page should reinforce that sentence or quietly support it. This is positioning, and it costs something real: you must choose what not to claim. A CV with fourteen emphases has none. My colleague's problem was not weak material — it was strong material with no editorial spine.

### Achievements, not responsibilities

The most common failure of senior CVs, and the one I keep committing myself: describing scope instead of outcomes. "Responsible for infrastructure across six sites" tells me what your job was. It does not tell me whether you were any good at it. The person who mismanaged that infrastructure for five years has the identical sentence available to them.

The fix is mechanical. For each role, ask: what changed because I was there, and what number proves it? Grew the storage estate tenfold while holding better than 99.9% availability. Cut disaster-recovery costs by seventy percent. Built identity management serving five thousand users across six sites. Led eight directly, influenced twenty. Those are claims a referee can confirm and an interviewer can probe — which is precisely what makes them credible.

Then take your top five skills and back each one with an outcome from the same page. A skills list without evidence is a wish list; a skills list where each item points at a result is a proof sketch.

### Two pages. Really.

Length was my final note. Senior careers generate material, and it is painful to cut projects you bled for. Cut them anyway. Two pages forces the prioritisation that the ten-second reality demands, and a reader who wants your 2009 achievements will ask. The third page is where good CVs go to die — it signals not richness of experience but reluctance to decide what matters, which is an odd message for an executive to send.

### Peer review works on careers too

The meta-lesson mattered more than any single rule. I have spent my working life around peer review — in science, where it guards the literature, and in engineering, where code review guards production. Both exist because authors are structurally the worst-placed people to evaluate their own work. We know what we meant, so we cannot see what we wrote.

A CV is the purest case of this. It is the document about which you are least objective, describing the subject you are most invested in. Reviewing my colleague's CV took me an hour; the flaws were obvious from the outside within minutes. Rereading my own afterwards, with the same checklist, was mildly humiliating: responsibilities dressed up as achievements, the best number buried mid-document, three emphases where there should have been one.

So the practical recipe, if you are overdue an overhaul: swap CVs with a peer you trust to be blunt — ideally someone outside your organisation, so they cannot fill gaps with context you failed to write down. Give them the checklist: ten-second test, one sticky message, aces in the top third, achievements with numbers, five evidenced skills, two pages. Ask them to say aloud, after ten seconds, what you appear to be. If their sentence is not the sentence you intended, the CV — not the reader — is wrong.

Then return the favour. Marking someone else's homework is the fastest way to learn what yours is worth.
