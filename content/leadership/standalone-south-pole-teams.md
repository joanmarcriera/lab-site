title: "Scott, Amundsen, and the myth of the motivated team"

Hiring for motivation and hoping expertise follows is a century-old mistake with a body count. The 1911 race to the South Pole — [Amundsen's success](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amundsen%27s_South_Pole_expedition), [Scott's disaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terra_Nova_Expedition) — is the classic case, and I find it uncomfortably applicable to how we build technology teams.

### Two teams, one goal, different foundations

On the surface the expeditions were similar: the same tactical objective, a clear chain of command in each. The differences sat underneath — the [side-by-side comparison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparison_of_the_Amundsen_and_Scott_expeditions) is worth an evening of any manager's time:

• **Expertise.** Amundsen assembled the highest expertise possible for the specific job — polar travel with dogs and skis. Scott assembled highly motivated people who, collectively, did not have enough of the right expertise.
• **Motivation type.** Personal reputation drove one venture; a mix of scientific and sporting goals drove the other. Different fuels burn differently under pressure.
• **Technology bets.** Tested dogs versus novel motor sledges. Betting on novel technology is legitimate — infrastructure people do it constantly. Doing it without a backup plan for its *known* risks is the mistake.
• **Economics.** Partial self-funding added personal financial risk, which quietly raises the price a leader is willing to pay to avoid turning back. Risk appetite is never just a personality trait; it is a balance sheet.

### Not luck

The perennial debate asks: were the contrasting outcomes just luck? My answer is no — with a twist. Luck would have been Scott *succeeding* on a first attempt with a new team, new technology, new everything. The disaster was the expected value. We call project failures "bad luck" far too often when they are actually unpriced risk arriving on schedule.

I see the software version of this regularly. A greenfield project staffed entirely with enthusiastic generalists, using a framework nobody has run in production, on a deadline set before the team existed. When it fails, the retrospective blames circumstances. The circumstances were visible on day one.

### The counterfactual that teaches the most

The best question you can ask of this case is the leader swap. What if Amundsen had led Scott's team, and Scott Amundsen's?

My take: Amundsen, sharing plans early with Scott's crew, would have let many opt out before departure — and the remainder would have bonded and prepared properly. Painful attrition up front; stronger expedition after. Meanwhile Scott, improvising in front of Amundsen's experts, might have faced mutiny at Madeira — the experienced hands would have recognised the gaps in the plan while there was still time to act.

Here is the part I find genuinely useful for engineering leadership: **that mutiny would have been the system working**. A team expert enough to refuse a fatally flawed plan, early enough to matter, is exactly the team you want. If your organisation's culture makes that refusal impossible — if pushing back is career-limiting — then you have optimised for Scott's outcome. There is a Harvard Business Review piece in my reading pile titled ["Is It Time for Mutiny?"](https://hbr.org/2013/04/is-it-time-to-stage-a-mutiny) which suggests I am not alone in this view.

### Managing experts is its own discipline

Years of leading technical teams have made me honest about high performers. They know the value of their skills and are not shy about it. They seek status, sometimes in small, insidious ways — special work patterns, flexibility others cannot get. They crave engagement and will move teams when they do not get it. They understand the organisation and its networks, and they will use both.

None of that makes them difficult. It makes them *legible*. An expert's demands are usually a well-priced invoice for value delivered; the manager's job is to decide openly what is affordable, rather than resent the negotiation. I once handled a classic dilemma — two experts wanting incompatible flexibility — with conditions: alternate the days off, and the senior one trains the team so his absence stops being a single point of failure. Trading privileges for de-risking is usually a deal experts accept, because it raises their standing while lowering your risk.

### High performance has a decay curve

One warning I pass on to every new manager: high performance is a journey, and it may crash at the end of a project. Teams that sprint brilliantly to a launch can fall apart the week after — exhaustion, purpose vacuum, the quiet exit of the people who carried the load. My checklist of ingredients for sustaining performance: clear goals, ruthless prioritisation, explicit values, agreed rules of the game, defined roles, working processes, and interpersonal wellbeing. Note how many of those are boring administrative choices. High performance is mostly logistics wearing a cape.

Amundsen's expedition looks, from a distance, like heroism. Up close it was preparation: the right experts, proven technology, honest planning, and a leader who understood that the goal was to come back. In infrastructure, as in polar travel, the epic stories usually describe the failures. The successes read like a supply manifest.

Think of the highest-stakes project on your roadmap right now: does the team have Amundsen's expertise, or Scott's enthusiasm — and which have you been rewarding in hiring?
