title: "They invented committees to slow me down. I fed them my best work."
date: 2026-07-20

There is a form of obstruction that never says no. It invents process instead.

I lived it for years. I'd push for something concrete — say, procedural change management for hardware, because every time we installed new disks somebody disconnected something that was still live — and the answer would be: "Write a business case. Present it to this group." A group that had just been created. Whose function nobody could state. That met once every six months. And that, on inspection, wasn't actually a change board or a decision body at all.

I complied a couple of times before I understood what it was: not governance, but a waiting room. Park the idea, park the person, and let the clock — in an institution where everyone's clock runs out — do the rest.

Then I changed strategy, and I recommend it to anyone stuck in the same room:

• I overcommitted. When the request was obviously busywork, I did my best work anyway. Every invented hoop got a presentation so thorough it embarrassed the hoop. Obstruction relies on you getting tired; enthusiasm ruins it.
• I converted audiences into networks. Every committee full of people who "weren't the deciders" was still a room full of people worth knowing. I stopped treating presentations as gates and started treating them as introductions.
• I used their own definition against them. "Present it to this group in two months, they'll decide." — "We're not a decision body." — "Then presenting to you is an acknowledgement, not a request. I'll inform everyone and proceed." Nobody ever had an answer to that. If a body doesn't decide, it can't block; it can only be informed. Pick one.
• I pre-wired everything. Before any formal presentation, I'd already discussed the idea with every member individually — and bent the plan a little after each conversation so each person found something of theirs in it. By meeting day, the presentation wasn't a proposal. It was a summary of a consensus that already existed. To stop it, someone would have had to stand up and publicly oppose the thing they'd privately shaped.

The Japanese have a name for that last move: nemawashi — preparing the ground, one conversation at a time, so the meeting ratifies rather than debates. I didn't learn it from a book. I learned it from being sent to committees that didn't exist.

The endgame was quiet: the people who had been routing me through inventions had nothing left to route me through. And that freed me to spend my time where the actual work was — with human resources, on identity, on the things that outlasted all of us.

Two lessons, honestly earned:

• Process used as a weapon dissolves when you take it more seriously than its authors do.
• The decision was never in the meeting. It's made one conversation at a time, before the agenda is even printed. If you only show up to the meeting, you've already lost.

Where have you seen process invented to avoid saying no out loud?
